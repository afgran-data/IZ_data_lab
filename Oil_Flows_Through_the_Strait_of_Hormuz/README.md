# Strait of Hormuz: Global Oil Trade Analysis

## Overview

The **Strait of Hormuz** is the world's most important oil chokepoint and one of the most strategically significant maritime passages in the global economy.

Despite being only about **21 miles (34 km)** wide at its narrowest point, this waterway connects the Persian Gulf to international markets and serves as a critical route for crude oil exports from major Gulf producers.

This project visualizes the global destinations of crude oil transported through the Strait of Hormuz and highlights how international energy trade has shifted toward Asia.

---

## What the Visualization Shows

The chart illustrates the destination of crude oil shipments passing through the Strait of Hormuz during **2024**.

According to the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA), approximately **14.3 million barrels of crude oil per day** passed through the strait in 2024 based on tanker-tracking data from Vortexa.

This represents a significant share of globally traded crude oil.

---

## Major Exporting Countries

Most oil moving through the Strait originates from Persian Gulf producers, including:

- Saudi Arabia
- Iraq
- United Arab Emirates
- Iran
- Kuwait
- Qatar

Saudi Arabia remains the largest exporter through the Strait of Hormuz.

---

## Major Importing Regions

Oil exported through the Strait is distributed worldwide, but demand is heavily concentrated in Asia.

### Asia

Approximately **84%** of all crude oil transported through the Strait of Hormuz in 2024 was destined for Asian markets.

Largest importers include:

| Country | Approximate Imports |
|---------|--------------------:|
| China | **4.8 million barrels/day** |
| India | **1.9 million barrels/day** |
| South Korea | **1.7 million barrels/day** |
| Japan | **1.5 million barrels/day** |

### Other Regions

- United States: **~0.5 million barrels/day**
- Europe: **~0.7 million barrels/day**

The relatively small share imported by the United States reflects the significant increase in domestic U.S. oil production over the past decade.

---

## Recent Developments

Preliminary data for **Q1 2025** indicate that total oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz remain close to **20 million barrels per day**, with China continuing to account for the largest share of imports, followed by India, Japan, and South Korea.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The Strait of Hormuz remains the world's most important oil chokepoint.
- The center of global oil demand has shifted decisively toward Asia.
- China is the largest importer of crude oil transported through the Strait.
- Gulf oil exporters increasingly depend on Asian economies as their primary customers.
- Any disruption to shipping through the Strait of Hormuz could significantly impact global energy markets and oil prices.

---

## Why It Matters

Because such a large portion of globally traded crude oil passes through a single narrow maritime corridor, geopolitical tensions, military conflict, or shipping disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz can have immediate effects on:

- Global oil prices
- Energy security
- Inflation
- International trade
- Financial markets

The Strait therefore remains one of the most strategically important locations in the global energy system.

## Purpose

This project aims to visualize the flow of crude oil through one of the world's most important energy chokepoints and illustrate how global oil trade has evolved in response to changing patterns of energy demand.

The analysis provides historical and geopolitical context for understanding why developments in the Strait of Hormuz continue to influence global energy markets and the broader world economy.
